**Title:** The Photographer Called to Confirm His Second Wedding

**Story:**
The wedding photographer called to confirm my husband's song request for his second wedding.

I picked up the phone assuming it was a spam call about our anniversary trip, the one my husband had been researching flights for every night after dinner for a month. Instead, a stranger introduced herself as a photographer working a wedding two states away, said she needed to confirm timing for the couple's first dance, and read off a song title I'd know anywhere, because it was ours. The exact track from our first dance, eleven years ago, in a ballroom neither of us had thought about publicly in a decade.

I asked for the groom's name, calm in a way that surprised me more than the question did. She gave it. It was his.

He wasn't traveling for work three weekends a month. He was building a second life two states away, complete with a fiancée who apparently loved the same song he'd once told me was "our thing," because he'd told her the same thing, word for word if the photographer's notes were any indication. I found the venue's website within the hour, a save-the-date page live for a wedding six weeks out, his photo cropped just enough to hide the ring he still wore in our kitchen every morning.

I didn't confront him over the phone. I called the venue directly instead, identified myself honestly as his legal wife, and asked one question: was he presenting a marriage certificate or divorce decree as part of their paperwork. The coordinator went quiet, then admitted no one had asked to see either, because most couples don't get asked unless something flags it first.

Something flagged it. The county clerk's office, once I filed the inquiry myself, confirmed there was no divorce on record, only a marriage — ours, still active, still legally binding, sitting in a filing cabinet two counties away from wherever he thought this new version of his life could quietly exist.

He came home that Friday already caught, the venue having called him first to cancel a booking they couldn't legally perform. He didn't get to explain. I'd already heard the explanation, second-hand, from a stranger holding a clipboard and a song list that used to only ever belong to us.

I kept the song. He doesn't get to. I play it now with the volume up, in a house that's finally only mine.

**Title:** The Surveyor's Stake Stood Inside My Flowerbed

**Story:**
The surveyor's new stake stood two feet inside my flowerbed.

My neighbor Gary hired him after eight years of vague comments about our shared fence line, comments I'd always brushed off as harmless if Gary hadn't started parking his utility trailer half on my side of the yard the same week the new stakes appeared, planted with surgical precision six inches past where the fence had stood since before either of us bought our houses.

The surveyor's report cited a "corrected boundary," backed by his own fresh measurements and a county file he said predated mine. I asked to see the original 1987 subdivision plat instead of his fresh numbers, and the county clerk pulled a document neither Gary nor his surveyor had bothered referencing, because the original developer had set a permanent brass benchmark disk at the true northeast corner, embedded four inches into concrete under what was now, in my yard, a forty-year-old oak tree nobody had thought to dig around.

Gary said the tree location proved nothing, that trees get planted anywhere. I told him fine, let's dig, and hired my own licensed surveyor to locate the disk using the county's original coordinates rather than Gary's surveyor's "corrected" ones. Forty minutes and one shovel later, the original benchmark sat exactly where the 1987 plat said it would, stamped with a serial number the county still had on file, two feet inside what Gary's fence was now claiming as his.

His surveyor, it turned out, had never actually located the historical marker at all — he'd measured from an assumed reference point Gary himself had shown him, a fence post Gary had moved three years earlier and conveniently forgotten to mention. Once the county reviewed both reports side by side, Gary's surveyor lost his state license pending investigation, and Gary lost the property line dispute he'd spent three months building a case for, plus the legal fees for a report the county now flagged as fraudulent in the property record.

I had the benchmark disk professionally capped and left visible instead of reburied, a small brass circle showing where the actual line has always been. Gary's new fence went back exactly six inches, right where the tree already knew it belonged.

I never planted that oak. Somebody else did, forty years ago, without knowing it would end up being the only witness that mattered.

**Title:** The Nurse Scanned My Wristband and Frowned

**Story:**
The nurse scanned my wristband and frowned.

I'd been prepped for a routine outpatient procedure, gown on, IV already started, when she scanned the barcode a second time, then a third, then stepped out without explaining why. Nobody prepped for surgery wants to watch their nurse leave mid-checklist looking at a screen like it had said something wrong.

She came back with another nurse and a laptop, and together they asked me to confirm my full name and birth date, which I did, calmly, the same way I had four times already at check-in. My wristband, it turned out, had scanned correctly into the system, but the system had linked it to a chart for a different procedure entirely, one involving anesthesia risks and a surgical site that had nothing to do with why I was there. Same first and last name. Same birth month and day. Different birth year, buried one field down in a form nobody had double-checked when admissions typed me in that morning.

The attending surgeon, when he finally came in, insisted the paperwork matched and that I was overthinking pre-op nerves, a phrase I've come to understand means "please stop asking questions before we start." I asked him directly whether he'd personally confirmed my procedure against my actual chart, not the one on the screen in the room. He hadn't. He assumed admissions had.

I refused to let anyone touch the IV again until someone printed my actual chart, the one with my real birth year on it, and held it next to the wristband barcode in front of me. It took forty minutes, three phone calls, and one visibly annoyed charge nurse before someone finally found the second patient, a man sharing my name down to the day and month, scheduled two rooms over for a completely different surgery under a chart that had briefly, silently, become mine.

They fixed it before either of us reached an operating room, but only because I'd refused to let "close enough" get me onto a table for someone else's procedure. My actual surgery happened four hours later than scheduled, uneventful, exactly as planned, exactly as it should have been from the start.

I still have the wristband. Not as a souvenir. As a reminder that a barcode is only as accurate as the last person who trusted it without checking.

**Title:** The Gallery Card Credited My Painting to Someone Else

**Story:**
The gallery card credited my painting to someone else's name.

I stood in front of a piece I'd spent four months on, layered in a technique only three people I know actually use, staring at a placard with my best friend Priya's name typed under a title I'd chosen myself the night I finished it. She'd submitted it to the regional emerging artist showcase as her own work, the one competition we'd talked about entering together for two years, the one she said she wasn't ready for yet.

I didn't say anything that night. I took a photo of the placard instead, and one of the canvas itself, close enough to catch a detail only I would recognize — a signature I never sign visibly, tucked under three layers of varnish in UV-reactive ink, a habit my grandmother taught me before she died, meant only for authentication, never meant to be needed against someone I trusted.

Priya accepted the runner-up prize two weeks later at the awards dinner, thanked her "creative process" in a five-minute speech, and never once mentioned whose studio the canvas had actually been painted in. I let her have the dinner. I let her have the applause. Competitions like this one required documentation for any piece advancing to the national round, though, including provenance verification for anything flagged as a potential dispute, and I'd already flagged mine three days after the local showcase, quietly, through the organization's ethics submission form, with photos of the UV signature attached.

The national committee's independent appraiser examined the canvas under blacklight during the verification period, the same one every advancing piece went through as standard procedure, not because anyone suspected Priya specifically. My initials came up clean and unmistakable, dated a full month before her submission paperwork existed. She was disqualified from the national round two days before it opened, her runner-up title from the regional show quietly revoked once the committee's findings were finalized.

She called me eleven times after the letter went out. I answered on the twelfth, not to yell, just to tell her the same thing my grandmother told me the day she taught me to sign under the varnish: some things you protect not because you expect betrayal, but because you're not surprised anymore when it happens.

I entered that painting into next year's national round myself. Under my own name, in plain ink, on top of the varnish this time. I don't need to hide it from anyone anymore.
